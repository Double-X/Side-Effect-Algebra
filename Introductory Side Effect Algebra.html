Scope

This article aims to briefly cover the elementary level side effect algebra mentioned in contents as a simple introduction for beginners.
Note that nondeterministic side effects are beyond the scope of this article in order to keep the scope small enough to be sufficiently easy.

Notation

Back To Contents

Let E be a side effect. Then:
1. E ( I , S ) means E is based on input set I and state set S.
2. E ( I , S ) = ∅ means E is effectively nullipotent for input set I and state set S.
3. E ( ∅ , S ) means E does not depend on any input set.
4. E ( I , ∅ ) means E does not depend on any state set.
5. E ( ∅ , ∅ ) means both of the above.
6. S o + E ( I , S ) = S n means the relevant state set is changed from S o to S n by applying E ( I , S ) .

For instance:
var x = S;
function E(I) {
    x += I;
};
As x = S + I after calling E, E is based on input set I and state set S.
On the other hand:
var x = S;
function E(I) {
    x = I;
};
E ( I , ∅ ) = ∅ when S = I and E ( I , ∅ ) ≠ ∅ when otherwise.
Another example:
var x = 0, y = S;
function E1() {
    x = y;
};
function E2() {
    y = 0;
};
E 1 ( ∅ , S ) and E 2 ( ∅ , ∅ ) represent side effects only depending on state set S and not depending on anything respectively.

Equality

Back To Contents

Definition

Let E 1 and E 2 be side effects. Then:
1. E 1 ( I , S ) = E 2 ( I , S ) ⇔ S o + E 1 ( I , S ) = S o + E 2 ( I , S )
2. E 1 ≡ E 2 ⇔ E 1 ( I , S ) = E 2 ( I , S ) ∀ I , S

For instance:
var x = S;
function E1(I) {
    x += I;
};
function E2(I) {
    x *= I;
};
E 1 ( 2 , 2 ) = E 2 ( 2 , 2 ) as they both lead to x = 4 , while E 1 ( 1 , 1 ) ≠ E 2 ( 1 , 1 ) as the former and latter lead to x = 2 and x = 1 respectively. So E 1 ≢ E 2 .
On the other hand:
var x = S;
function E1(I) {
    return x + I;
};
function E2(I) {
    return x * I;
};
E 1 ≡ E 2 ≡ ∅ even though they are completely different functions returning completely different values.

Properties

Side effect equalities are transitive, meaning that:
1. E 1 ( I , S ) = E 2 ( I , S ) ∧ E 2 ( I , S ) = E 3 ( I , S ) ⇒ E 1 ( I , S ) = E 3 ( I , S )
2. E 1 ≡ E 2 ∧ E 2 ≡ E 3 ⇒ E 1 ≡ E 3

Addition

Back To Contents

Definition

Let E 1 and E 2 be side effects. Then E 1 + E 2 means applying E 1 is immediately followed by applying E 2 .
For instance:
function E() {
    E1();
    E2();
};
function E() {
    E2(E1());
};
Here E ≡ E 1 + E 2 for both functions even though those functions are not themselves equivalent.

Properties

1. Side effect additions are associative, meaning that ( E 1 + E 2 ) + E 3 ≡ E 1 + ( E 2 + E 3 ) .
The below example illustrates this property:
var x = S;
function E1(I) {
    x += I;
};
function E2(I) {
    x *= I;
};
function E3(I) {
    x = Math.pow(x, I);
};
Even though both ( E 1 + E 2 ) + E 3 and E 1 + ( E 2 + E 3 ) leads to x = [ ( S + I ) I ] I , the former means applying the combined side effects of applying " E 1 immediately followed by applying E 2 " will be immediately followed by applying E 3 , while the latter means applying E 1 will be immediately followed by applying the combined side effects of applying " E 2 immediately followed by applying E 3 ".
In short, the essential difference is just the ordering of how we reason about those side effects, which does not affect their actual execution orders nor dependencies.

2. Side effect additions are not commutative in general, meaning that E 1 + E 2 ≢ E 2 + E 1 .
For instance:
var x = 0;
function E() {
    // Contents are here...
    //
};
function E1() {
    x += 2;
};
function E2() {
    x %= 2;
};
If E ≡ E 1 + E 2 , x will be equal to 0; If E ≡ E 2 + E 1 , x will be equal to 2.

3. Side effect additions are not distributive in general, meaning that ( E 1 + E 2 ) ( I , S ) ≠ E 1 ( I , S ) + E 2 ( I , S ) .
For instance:
var x = y = S;
function E(I) {
    E1(I);
    E2(x);
};
function E1(I) {
    x += I;
};
function E2(I) {
    y *= I;
};
Here ( E 1 + E 2 ) ( I , S ) leads to x = S + I and y = S ( S + I ) respectively, while E 1 ( I , S ) + E 2 ( I , S ) leads to x = S + I and y = S I respectively.
However, ( E 1 + E 2 ) ( I , S ) = E 1 ( I , S ) + E 2 ( I 0 , S 0 ) .

Additive Identity

Nullipotence is the additive identity, meaning that ∅ + E ≡ E ≡ E + ∅ .

Theorem

Identity Conservation Theorem
E + E 1 ≡ E + E 2 ⇐ E 1 ≡ E 2 ⇒ E 1 + E ≡ E 2 + E
Proof:
Let the relevant state set right before applying E 1 or E 2 be S.
As E 1 ≡ E 2 , they must lead to be exact same relevant state set, S 0 , right after applying either of them.
Therefore the relevant state set right after applying E must be the same regardless of whether it immediately follows E 1 or E 2 .
Similarly, if S is obtained right after applying E, then S 0 will still be obtained right after applying E 1 or E 2 .
So the Identity Conservation Theorem holds.
The converses will be covered in the Subtraction section.

Subtraction

Back To Contents

Additive Inverse

E 1 + E 2 ≡ ∅ ⇔ E 2 ≡ - E 1 ⇏ E 1 ≡ - E 2
In other words, E 2 is the additive inverse of E 1 while the converse is not necessarily true.
On the other hand, if they are indeed additive inverses of each other, then they are symmetry side effects.
Therefore, E 1 - E 2 means applying E 1 is immediately followed by applying the additive inverse of E 2 .
It implies that it is an invalid operation if no such side effect exists.

For instance:
var x = S;
function E(I) {
    x *= I;
};
function E1(I) {
    x += I;
};
function E2(I) {
    x -= I;
};
To evaluate E - E 1 , the additive inverse of E 1 must be found first.
As E 1 + E 2 ≡ ∅ , E 2 is the additive inverse of E 1 .
Therefore E - E 1 ≡ E + E 2 .
In this case, it leads to x = I ( S - 1 ) .

Properties

1. Some side effects do not have additive inverses.
For instance, the below side effect does not have any:
var x = S;
function E(I) {
    x = I;
};
The additive inverse of E would be to set the value of x to be the one right before applying E, which is S in this case.
However, right after applying E, the previous value of x will be lost forever, making the additive inverse of E impossible to work in reality.
Therefore, the additive inverse of E is effectively nonexistent.
Note that this can still apply even for side effects being additive inverses of some other side effects.
For instance:
var x = S;
function E1(I) {
    x = Math.pow(x, 2);
};
function E2(I) {
    x = Math.sqrt(x);
};
While E 2 + E 1 ≡ ∅ as ( | S | ) 2 ≡ | S | ∧ ( - | S | ) 2 ≡ - | S | , E 1 does not have an additive inverse as | S | 2 ≡ ( - | S | ) 2 , meaning that there is no way to reliably recover the state set right before applying E 1 right after applying it.

2. In general, if E does not have an additive inverse, then E 1 + E ≡ E 2 + E ⇏ E 1 ≡ E 2 .
For instance:
var x = 0;
function E1() {
    x += 2;
};
function E2() {
    x++;
};
function E() {
    x = 3;
};
While E 1 + E ≡ E 2 + E as they both lead to x = 3 , E 1 ≢ E 2 as the former leads to x = 2 while the latter leads to x = 1 .
However, E + E 3 ≡ ∅ ∧ E 1 + E ≡ E 2 + E ⇒ E 1 ≡ E 2 .
Proof: E 1 + E ≡ E 2 + E
⇒ E 1 + E + E 3 ≡ E 2 + E + E 3 (Identity Conservation Theorem)
⇒ E 1 + ∅ ≡ E 2 + ∅
⇒ E 1 ≡ E 2 (Additive Identity)
Therefore this property holds.

Theorem

1. Mutual Inverse Theorem
E 2 ≡ - E 1 ∧ E 3 ≡ - E 2 ⇒ E 1 ≡ E 3
Proof: E 2 ≡ - E 1
⇒ E 1 + E 2 ≡ E 1 - E 1 (Identity Conservation Theorem)
⇒ E 1 + E 2 + E 3 ≡ E 1 - E 1 + E 3 (Identity Conservation Theorem)
⇒ E 1 + ∅ ≡ ∅ + E 3
⇒ E 1 ≡ E 3 (Additive Identity)
So the Mutual Inverse Theorem holds.
Note that this theorem implies that side effect additive inverses are antitransitive.

2. Identity Conservation Theorem
E + E 1 ≡ E + E 2 ⇒ E 1 ≡ E 2
Proof:
Let the relevant state set right after applying E be S and that right after applying either E 1 or E 2 be S 0 .
It means that applying both E 1 and E 2 will change the relevant state set from S to S 0 .
Therefore, E 1 and E 2 are effectively the same.
So the Identity Conservation Theorem holds.

Orthogonality

Back To Contents

Notation

Two side effects are orthogonal to each other if they are not identical and their additions are commutative, meaning that E 1 ⊥ E 2 ⇔ E 1 ≢ E 2 ∧ E 1 + E 2 ≡ E 2 + E 1 .
For instance:
var x = S1, y = S2;
function E1() {
    x += I;
};
function E2(I) {
    y += I;
};
Here E 1 ⊥ E 2 because:
1. E 1 and E 2 changes the values of x and y respectively, meaning that E 1 ≢ E 2 .
2. Both E 1 + E 2 and E 2 + E 1 leads to x = S 1 + I and y = S 2 + I , meaning that E 1 + E 2 ≡ E 2 + E 1 .

Nullipotence

Back To Contents

Involution

2 E ≡ ∅ means E is involutory.
For instance:
var x = S;
function E() {
    x = -x;
};
As x = S after calling E twice, which is the same as not calling anything at all, 2 E ≡ ∅ .
Note that nullipotence implies involution.

Periodic

A side effect being nullipotent if applied a certain number of times consecutively is periodic, meaning that P ( E ) = n ⇒ n E ≡ ∅ ⇒ P ( E ) ≤ n ∀ n ∈ ℕ .
For instance:
var x = 0;
function E() {
    x += 1;
    x %= 60;
};
As x = 0 after calling E 60 times, which is the same as not calling anything at all, 60 E ≡ ∅ ⇒ P ( E ) ≤ 60 .
Note that the above still holds even if 60 is replaced by 120, so n E ≡ ∅ ⇒ P ( E ) ≤ n instead of n E ≡ ∅ ⇒ P ( E ) = n .
Also:
1. P ( E ) = 1 ⇔ E ≡ ∅
2. P ( E ) = 2 ⇔ E is involutory.
3. P ( E ) = ∞ ⇔ E is not periodic.

Theorem

1. Period Modulo Theorem
( m P ( E ) + n ) E ≡ n E ∀ m , n ∈ ℕ ∪ { 0 } ∧ n < P ( E ) < ∞
Proof:
( m P ( E ) + n ) E
≡ m ( P ( E ) E ) + n E
≡ m ∅ + n E
≡ n E (Additive Identity)
So the Period Modulo Theorem holds.

2. Composite Period Theorem
If E only consists of m i times of E i ∀ i ∈ [ 1 , n ] ∧ E i ⊥ E j ∀ j ∈ [ 1 , n ] \ { i } ∧ P ( E i ) < ∞ , then P ( E ) ≤ the smallest integer not smaller than ∏ i = 1 n p i P ( E i ) m i n ≤ ∏ i = 1 n P ( E i ) ∃ p i ∈ ℕ .
Proof:
As every side effect constituting E is orthogonal to each other, using their commutative property:
E ≡ ∑ i = 1 n m i E i
( P ( E ) + 1 ) ∑ i = 1 n m i E i ≡ ∑ i = 1 n m i E i (Period Modulo Theorem)
( P ( E ) + 1 ) m i E i ≡ m i E i ∀ i ∈ [ 1 , n ]
( p i P ( E i ) + m i ) E i ≡ m i E i ∃ p i ∈ ℕ (Period Modulo Theorem)
( P ( E ) + 1 ) m i ≤ p i P ( E i ) + m i
P ( E ) ≤ p i P ( E i ) m i
P ( E ) n ≤ ∏ i = 1 n p i P ( E i ) m i
P ( E ) ≤ the smallest integer not smaller than ∏ i = 1 n p i P ( E i ) m i n
Setting p i as m i P ( E i ) n - 1 ,
∏ i = 1 n p i P ( E i ) m i n
= ∏ i = 1 n m i P ( E i ) n - 1 P ( E i ) m i n
= ∏ i = 1 n P ( E i ) n n
= ( ∏ i = 1 n P ( E i ) ) n n
= ∏ i = 1 n P ( E i )
So the Composite Period Theorem holds.
Note that by setting m i and P ( E i ) as 1 and 2 respectively, this theorem implies that a side effect is involutory if it only consists of involutory side effects that are all orthogonal to each other.

Multiplicity

Back To Contents

Notation

A side effect is said to have a multiplicity if applying it a certain number of times is equivalent to applying it once, meaning that M ( E ) = n ⇒ ( n + 1 ) E ≡ E ⇒ M ( E ) ≤ n ∀ n ∈ ℕ .

For instance:
var x = S;
function E(I) {
    x = I;
};
Here 2 E ≡ ( 1 + 1 ) E ≡ E ⇒ M ( E ) ≤ 1
Note that the above still holds even if n > 1 , so ( n + 1 ) E ≡ E ⇒ M ( E ) ≤ n instead of ( n + 1 ) E ≡ E ⇒ M ( E ) = n .
Also:
1. n = 1 implies idempotence.
2. n = ∞ implies the side effect does not have a multiplicity.
For instance:
var x = S;
function E(I) {
    x += I;
};
As x = S + n I after calling E n times, E does not have a multiplicity.

Properties

E ≢ ∅ ∧ M ( E ) = 1 ⇒ P ( E ) = ∞
Proof:
As an idempotent side effect means applying it twice consecutively is equivalent to applying it once, so does applying it an arbitrary number of times.
On the other hand, if it were also periodic, it would mean applying it a certain number of times would lead to nullipotence.
If both of the above would hold, it would mean the side effect itself were nullipotent, which is a contradiction.
Therefore this property holds.

Theorem

1. Period-Multiplicity Theorem
M ( E ) ≤ P ( E )
Proof:
( P ( E ) + 1 ) E
≡ E (Period Modulo Theorem)
⇒ M ( E ) ≤ P ( E )
So the Period-Multiplicity Theorem holds.
Note that it implies that a side effect not having a multiplicity must be not periodic as M ( E ) = ∞ ⇒ P ( E ) = ∞ .

2. Multiplicity Modulo Theorem
( m M ( E ) + n ) E ≡ n E ∀ m , n ∈ ℕ ∧ n ≤ M ( E ) < ∞
Proof:
( m M ( E ) + n ) E
≡ [ ( m - 1 ) M ( E ) + M ( E ) + 1 + n - 1 ] E
≡ ( m - 1 ) M ( E ) E + ( M ( E ) + 1 ) E + ( n - 1 ) E
≡ ( m - 1 ) M ( E ) E + E + ( n - 1 ) E
≡ [ ( m - 1 ) M ( E ) + n ] E
≡ … ≡ n E
So the Multiplicity Modulo Theorem holds.

3. Composite Multiplicity Theorem
It and its proof are exactly the same as those of the Composite Period Theorem, only with P and Period Modulo Theorem replaced by M and Multiplicity Modulo Theorem respectively.
Note that by setting both m i and M ( E i ) as 1, this theorem implies that a side effect is idempotent if it only consists of idempotent side effects that are all orthogonal to each other.
